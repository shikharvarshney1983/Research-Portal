📚 Banking Sector Glossary & Peer Comparison

Key Banking Metrics Explained

📊 Net Interest Income (NII)

Definition: Difference between interest earned on loans/investments and interest paid on deposits/borrowings. This is the PRIMARY revenue source for banks (equivalent to "Revenue" for manufacturing companies).

Formula: NII = Interest Income - Interest Expense
Why Important: Core banking profitability indicator. Higher NII = better lending efficiency and pricing power.
Good Range: 10-15% YoY growth; sustainable expansion indicates healthy loan book growth + NIM improvement.

💰 Net Profit (PAT - Profit After Tax)

Definition: Final profit after deducting all expenses (operating costs, provisions for bad loans, taxes) from total income (NII + Other Income like fees, treasury gains).

Formula: PAT = (NII + Other Income) - Operating Expenses - Provisions - Tax
Why Important: Bottom-line profitability. Shows actual earnings available to shareholders.
Good Range: 12-18% YoY growth for mature banks; 20%+ for high-growth banks.

📈 EPS (Earnings Per Share)

Definition: Net profit divided by total number of shares outstanding. Shows per-share profitability.

Formula: EPS = Net Profit (PAT) ÷ Number of Shares
Why Important: Used for P/E ratio calculation and valuation. Higher EPS growth = higher shareholder value creation.
Good Range: 12-18% CAGR for mature banks; dilution from equity raises temporarily reduces EPS.

⚠️ Gross NPA (GNPA) & Net NPA (NNPA)

Definition: Non-Performing Assets - loans where borrowers haven't paid principal/interest for 90+ days.

Formulas:
• Gross NPA Ratio = (Gross NPAs ÷ Gross Advances) × 100
• Net NPA Ratio = (Gross NPAs - Provisions) ÷ (Gross Advances - Provisions) × 100
Why Important: Asset quality indicator. Lower = better credit underwriting and collection.
Good Range: GNPA <2%, NNPA <0.5% = excellent. GNPA >4% = stressed portfolio.

💎 ROE (Return on Equity)

Definition: Net profit as percentage of average shareholder equity. Shows how efficiently bank uses shareholders' capital.

Formula: ROE = (Net Profit ÷ Average Shareholder Equity) × 100
Why Important: PRIMARY profitability metric for banks (more important than ROCE - see below). High ROE = efficient capital deployment.
Good Range: 15-18% = excellent; 12-15% = good; <10% = weak. Top banks (HDFC, ICICI) achieve 16-19%.

🏦 CASA Ratio (Current + Savings Account)

Definition: Percentage of total deposits in low-cost current and savings accounts (vs high-cost term deposits).

Formula: CASA Ratio = (Current Account Deposits + Savings Account Deposits) ÷ Total Deposits × 100
Why Important: Lower funding cost = higher NIM. CASA deposits cost 3-4% vs term deposits 6-7%.
Good Range: >45% = excellent (HDFC Bank ~47%); 35-45% = good; <30% = weak franchise.

📊 NIM (Net Interest Margin)

Definition: Net interest income as percentage of average earning assets (loans + investments). Shows lending profitability.

Formula: NIM = (NII ÷ Average Earning Assets) × 100
Why Important: Core profitability metric. Higher NIM = better pricing power and asset-liability management.
Good Range: 3.5-4.5% = excellent (small/mid banks); 3.0-3.5% = good (large banks); <3% = compressed margins.

💸 Cost of Funds

Definition: Average interest rate bank pays on deposits and borrowings.

Formula: Cost of Funds = (Total Interest Expense ÷ Average Interest-Bearing Liabilities) × 100
Why Important: Lower cost = higher NIM. Driven by CASA ratio and deposit mix.
Good Range: 4.0-4.5% = excellent; 4.5-5.5% = average; >6% = high-cost funding (risky).

🎯 ROA (Return on Assets)

Definition: Net profit as percentage of total assets. Shows efficiency in deploying total asset base.

Formula: ROA = (Net Profit ÷ Average Total Assets) × 100
Why Important: Asset efficiency metric. Banks operate on low ROA (1-2%) due to high leverage (unlike 8-12% ROA for manufacturing).
Good Range: 1.5-2.0% = excellent; 1.0-1.5% = good; <0.8% = weak asset deployment.

⚙️ Cost-to-Income Ratio (Efficiency Ratio)

Definition: Operating expenses as percentage of total income (NII + Other Income). Shows operational efficiency.

Formula: Cost-to-Income = (Operating Expenses ÷ Total Income) × 100
Why Important: Lower ratio = more efficient operations and higher profitability.
Good Range: <45% = excellent (HDFC Bank ~40%); 45-55% = good; >60% = inefficient (high branch/employee costs).

📖 Book Value (BV) Per Share

Definition: Net worth (shareholder equity) per share. Shows intrinsic accounting value per share.

Formula: Book Value = (Total Equity - Preference Shares) ÷ Number of Shares
Why Important: Used for P/B ratio calculation. Growing BV = wealth creation for shareholders.
Good Range: 12-18% CAGR book value growth = strong compounding. P/B ratio 2.5-4x indicates market's confidence in franchise quality.

💹 P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book Value)

Definition: Stock price divided by book value per share. Shows market's valuation premium over accounting value.

Formula: P/B Ratio = Market Price Per Share ÷ Book Value Per Share
Why Important: PRIMARY valuation metric for banks (P/E less relevant due to provisioning volatility). High P/B = quality franchise with high ROE.
Good Range: P/B 3-4x for ROE 16-18% banks (HDFC, ICICI); P/B 1.5-2.5x for ROE 12-15% banks; P/B <1x = distressed.

❌ Why ROCE is Irrelevant for Banks

⚠️ ROCE (Return on Capital Employed) - Not Applicable for Banks & Financial Institutions

ROCE Formula: ROCE = EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax) ÷ Capital Employed × 100
Capital Employed = Equity + Debt

Why ROCE Doesn't Work for Banks:

  • 1. Debt IS the Business: Banks' liabilities (deposits, borrowings) are NOT debt in traditional sense - they are raw material for lending business. Manufacturing companies use debt to fund operations; banks USE deposits (liabilities) AS THE PRODUCT to generate interest income. Treating deposits as "debt" in ROCE distorts the metric.
  • 2. Capital Structure Not Comparable: Banks operate at 10-15x leverage (Total Assets ÷ Equity). A typical manufacturing company operates at 1-3x leverage. ROCE compares equity + debt returns, but for banks, this denominator becomes artificially huge (₹100 equity + ₹1,000 deposits = ₹1,100 capital employed). This makes ROCE appear very low (2-4%) even for highly profitable banks.
  • 3. EBIT Doesn't Exist for Banks: Banks don't report "Earnings Before Interest & Tax" - interest expense is CORE to their P&L (it's the cost of raw material - deposits). You can't separate "operating profit" from "interest cost" for banks like you can for Tata Motors or Reliance. The concept of EBIT breaks down.
  • 4. Regulatory Capital Framework: Banks are regulated by capital adequacy ratios (CAR - Tier 1, Tier 2 capital), not traditional debt/equity. RBI mandates minimum 11.5% CAR. ROCE ignores this regulatory framework entirely, making it meaningless for bank analysis.
  • 5. ROE & ROA Are Superior Metrics: For banks, use ROE (return on equity - shows shareholder value creation) and ROA (return on assets - shows asset deployment efficiency). These are industry-standard metrics globally for banking analysis. ROCE is used for capital-intensive industries (steel, auto, infrastructure) where debt financing is discretionary - not for banks where deposits ARE the business model.

Bottom Line: Ignore ROCE for banks. Focus on ROE (primary), ROA (secondary), NIM (profitability), GNPA (asset quality), CASA (funding efficiency), and P/B ratio (valuation). ROCE is designed for asset-heavy manufacturing/infrastructure companies, NOT for lending institutions.

🎯 GARP Analysis: Federal Bank Through Peter Lynch's Lens

📖 What is GARP (Growth At Reasonable Price)?

Peter Lynch's Philosophy: GARP combines the best of value and growth investing. Lynch, the legendary Fidelity Magellan Fund manager (1977-1990, delivering 29.2% CAGR), believed in buying growth companies at reasonable valuations. His famous quote: "Buy what you know, and if you like the store, chances are you'll love the stock."

Core Principle: Find companies with strong earnings growth (12-25% annually) trading at P/E ratios lower than or equal to their growth rates. The PEG Ratio (P/E ÷ Growth Rate) is the key metric: PEG < 1.0 = Undervalued, PEG = 1.0 = Fair Value, PEG > 1.5 = Overvalued.

Lynch's 6 Stock Categories:

  • • Slow Growers: Large, mature companies; 2-4% growth (utilities, conglomerates)
  • • Stalwarts: Large, reliable companies; 8-12% growth (P&G, Coca-Cola type)
  • • Fast Growers: Small-to-mid companies; 20-50% growth (Lynch's favorites!)
  • • Cyclicals: Performance tied to economic cycles (auto, steel, airlines)
  • • Turnarounds: Distressed companies recovering (high risk, high reward)
  • • Asset Plays: Companies with undervalued assets (real estate, natural resources)

Category: STALWART (with Fast Grower characteristics)

Rationale: Federal Bank falls between Stalwart and Fast Grower categories. With ₹70,340 Cr market cap (mid-cap in Indian banking), it's too large to be a pure "fast grower" but exhibits growth rates (13-17% EPS growth) faster than typical stalwarts (8-12%). Lynch called these "Stalwarts with Growth Kickers" - his second-favorite category after fast growers.

  • • Consistent profitability (ROE 13-15% stable)
